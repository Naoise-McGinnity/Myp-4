An investigation to see how the area of a parachute affects the time an object takes to fall.

How does the surface area of a parachute affect the time a 0.02 kg takes to fall 2.69 m?

As an object — with or without a parachute — free falls, at first, only its own weight is acting on it and it is accelerating at 10 ms⁻². As it accelerates, air resistance becomes a factor, slowing the acceleration until the object reaches terminal velocity, where air resistance and the objects weight are balanced.

Hypothesis

I think that, as you increase the area of the parachute, the object will take longer to fall. I think this because, while the weight increase is almost negligible, air resistance is proportional to both surface area and velocity, so, as surface area increases, the velocity required for the air resistance to reach balance with the weight of the object decreases. As a result, the terminal velocity of the object decreases, thus increasing the time it takes to fall.

Variables

Independent Variable (IV): The surface area of the parachute (m²). This is the variable I am changing.

Dependent Variable (DV): The time taken for the object to fall (s). This is the variable I am measuring.

Control Variables (CV): To ensure a fair test, the following variables were kept constant:

Variable Value Reason for Control
Mass of object 0.02 kg Changing the mass changes the Weight force (W=mg). If the weight changes, the net force acting on the object changes, which would affect the time to fall regardless of the parachute area.
Drop Height 2.69 m If the object falls from a different height, the time taken will naturally be different (s = ut + ½at²). We must keep distance constant to compare the times fairly.
Release Method Dropped from rest Giving the object an initial push (initial velocity) would reduce the fall time. It must be dropped with u = 0 ms⁻¹.
Environmental Conditions Indoors (No wind) Air currents or drafts could apply extra horizontal or vertical force to the parachute, interfering with the drag caused by the parachute’s area.
Shape of Parachute Square Different shapes would affect how air flows around the parachute and thus how much air resistance there is.

Method

  1. Cut an 80 × 80 cm square from a large bin bag using scissors and a metre ruler.
  2. Cut 4 pieces of string, each exactly 30 cm long.
  3. Attach the 20 g weight to the corners of the parachute using the strings.
  4. Hold the parachute at a height of 2.69 m from the ground.
  5. Drop the object and simultaneously start the stopwatch. Stop the stopwatch when the weight hits the floor.
  6. Record the time in the results table.
  7. Repeat steps 4–6 two more times to calculate an average (reducing random error).
  8. Detach the strings and trim the parachute by 10 cm on each side (to 70 × 70 cm).
  9. Re-attach the strings and repeat the experiment until the parachute has been trimmed to 0 × 0 cm.

Risk Assessment

Hazard Risk Precaution
Falling Object (20 g mass) The mass is falling from 2.69 m. If it hits someone’s head or foot, it could cause injury/bruising. Ensure the area directly below the drop zone is clear of people.
Working at Height To reach 2.69 m, We had to stand on a table. There is a risk of falling off and causing injury. Do not lean to far over the edge of the table.

Conclusions

Based on my graph, as the area of the parachute increases, the time the weight takes to fall increases as well. The relationship seems to match the graph of y = √x pretty closely.

This result occurs because the Air Resistance (Drag) acting on the parachute is proportional to its Surface Area.

  1. As we increased the area, the parachute collided with more air particles, creating a larger upward Drag force.
  2. The downward Weight force remained constant (W = mg).
  3. Because the Drag was higher, it balanced the Weight at a much lower speed (terminal velocity).
  4. Since the object fell at a lower average speed, it took more time to cover the 2.69 m distance.

Evaluation

  1. Reaction Time

    The most significant source of random error in this investigation was the reliance on human reaction time when using the stopwatch.

  • Human reaction time is typically around 0.2 s. For the smaller parachutes, where the fall time was very short, this delay accounts for a large percentage of the total measured time. This caused fluctuations in the results that were difficult to smooth out even with averages.
  • To eliminate this error, we could use light gates connected to a data logger to automatically start and stop the timer. Alternatively, we could record the drop using a high frame-rate video and analyze the footage frame-by-frame to determine the exact start and stop times.

  1. Parachute Geometry

    A source of measurement error arose from the materials used.

  • The bin bag used to construct the parachutes was not perfectly rectangular; it tapered (became thinner) at one end. This meant that although we calculated the area as a perfect square (e.g., 80 × 80 cm), the actual surface area was likely smaller than calculated. This horizontal shift in the independent variable means our data points on the graph are slightly inaccurate.
  • Next time, I would use a large roll of graph paper or measuring grid to cut the initial square, ensuring perfectly 90-degree corners and exact dimensions before attaching the strings.

  1. Initial Free-fall Phase

    A significant source of error arose from the release method and the acceleration of the weight before the parachute deployed.

  • Because the canopy and weight were held together at the release point, the weight initially fell in free fall until the strings went taut and the parachute caught the air. As the parachute size increased, the slack or distance the weight had to travel before the canopy fully inflated and provided tension increased. This means the larger parachutes allowed the weight to accelerate unperturbed for a longer distance than the smaller parachutes. Consequently, the measured time for larger parachutes is slightly shorter than it theoretically should be, as they spent a larger fraction of the drop in free-fall compared to the smaller sizes.
  • To ensure drag acts immediately upon release, the object should be dropped with the strings already fully extended. Alternatively, the drop height should be measured from the point where the strings become taut, not the release point.
